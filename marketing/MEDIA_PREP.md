3. **"Built for longshoremen, by someone who listened to them"** - This was not built in a boardroom. It came from watching these workers struggle with notebooks and spreadsheets, doing math at 4am after a graveyard shift.

## 2. ORIGIN STORY

### The Question You Will Always Be Asked

"So how did you come up with this idea?"

### The Story (Practice This Until Natural)

**The Short Version (30 seconds):**

"I was watching a longshoreman try to figure out if his paycheck was right. He had a notebook with handwritten rates, a calculator, and a pay stub with 40 line items. This was a guy making good money - but he had no idea if it was the RIGHT money. I thought, there has to be a better way. Turns out there wasn't. So I built one."

**The Longer Version (2-3 minutes, for deeper interviews):**

"Longshoremen have one of the most complex pay systems I've ever seen. You've got base rates that change by shift - day, night, graveyard. Then they change again on weekends. Then you add job differentials - a mechanic makes more than a driver, who makes more than general labor. Then you layer on location variations - Centennial terminal runs 9-hour shifts, Vanterm runs 8. Then subjobs. Then overtime rules.

I watched a worker try to reconcile his pay stub one night. He was cross-referencing a handwritten notebook, a union rate sheet, and doing mental math to figure out if one shift was coded right. This is a guy who just worked a 9-hour shift moving containers. The last thing he should be doing at midnight is forensic accounting.

And when I asked others, they all had the same problem. Some just gave up and trusted payroll. Others obsessed over it and still got it wrong. The ones who tracked well did it in Excel spreadsheets so complicated they couldn't share them with anyone.

I thought - this is a solvable problem. Complex pay rules, yes. But computers are good at complex rules. The workers just need something that speaks their language, that knows their world. That's what PORTPAL is."

### Personal Connection Points

- "These are people who make the global economy move. Literally. Every product on every shelf touched a port somewhere."
- "There's a pride in this work. Union work, good wages, pension security. But the complexity of the system can undermine that."
- "I didn't grow up wanting to build payroll software. I grew up respecting people who work hard. This is just the tool they needed."

### The "Aha" Moment (Choose the one that fits the interview)

**Option A - The Notebook Story:**
"I saw a guy with a spiral notebook that had been through a dozen rain storms. Pages falling out. His whole financial life held together with rubber bands. And I thought - this guy operates million-dollar cranes, and we're making him track his pay with a wet notebook?"

**Option B - The Numbers Story:**
"Someone told me there were 'a lot of different rates.' I thought maybe 20, 30. Then I started mapping it out. 42 jobs. 3 shifts. 24 locations. Weekday vs weekend. Subjobs. I ended up with 994 unique combinations. That's when I knew this wasn't about being organized - it was about being overwhelmed."

**Option C - The Trust Story:**
"A worker told me he once found a $200 error on his check. But he couldn't prove it because he didn't have records. He had to let it go. $200. That's someone's groceries. That's when I knew this mattered."

## 3. PROBLEM FRAMING

### How to Explain the Complexity (Without Losing People)

**The Simplest Explanation:**

"Imagine if your paycheck changed every single day. Not just based on hours - based on which door you walked through, which truck you drove, whether it was Tuesday or Saturday, and whether the sun was up. Now imagine keeping track of all that with a notebook. That's what longshoremen deal with."

### Relatable Analogies

**The Tax Return Analogy:**
"You know how most people dread doing their taxes once a year? Imagine doing that math every two weeks, for every shift, where a single wrong code means $50 missing from your check. That's the longshore pay problem."

**The Frequent Flyer Analogy:**
"It's like trying to track airline miles, but across 42 different airlines, where each one has different rules for weekends, holidays, premium seats, and partner flights. And you have to reconcile it all by hand."

**The Restaurant Bill Analogy:**
"Imagine splitting a restaurant bill with 20 people, but everyone ordered different things, some people have discounts, others are paying double for prime seating, and the receipt is in a different language. That's a longshoreman's pay stub."

**The Gig Economy Comparison:**
"Uber drivers have apps that track everything automatically. Doordash dashers see their earnings in real-time. But longshoremen - who can make $150,000 a year - are stuck with notebooks and spreadsheets. It's backwards."

## 5. INDUSTRY INSIGHTS

### Interesting Facts About Longshoremen

**The Money:**
- Top earners can make $150,000+ annually
- Average longshoreman pay in the US/Canada exceeds $100,000 with overtime
- The ILWU contract covers 29 seaports on the West Coast
- 15,000 ILWU dockworkers averaged $171,000 in 2019

**The Work:**
- They move 90% of everything Americans buy
- A single container crane can move 30+ containers per hour
- Port operations run 24/7/365 - three shifts, every day
- Workers often don't know their job until hours before the shift

**The System:**
- Dispatching happens through a union hall, not traditional hiring
- "Casual" workers wait for their chance to become "Class B" - then eventually "Class A"
- Pension is based on hours worked - miss the threshold, lose the credit
- The job classification system dates back decades

**The Culture:**
- Strong union identity - these are union families, often multi-generational
- Port work is physical, skilled, and respected
- Workers take pride in knowing the waterfront
- There's a camaraderie that comes from shift work and shared conditions

### Common Misconceptions to Correct

**"Longshoremen are overpaid for unskilled work"**

REALITY: "These jobs require extensive training, certification, and skill. Operating a ship gantry crane requires precision work 100 feet in the air. HD Mechanics maintain million-dollar equipment. And yes, the pay is good - but the hours are grueling, the conditions are tough, and the work keeps the economy moving. This isn't unskilled labor."

**"They just need a simple spreadsheet"**

REALITY: "I used to think that too. Then I mapped 994 different pay combinations. Spreadsheets work when rules are simple. The longshoreman pay system is not simple. It's shift differentials, job classifications, location-based hours, weekend premiums, overtime rules, and pension thresholds - all interacting."

**"Technology is replacing dock workers"**

REALITY: "Automation is a complicated topic in this industry. The recent ILA contract actually limits certain automated technologies. But here's what I know: whether the port has 100% human workers or 50% automation, the workers who are there still need to be paid correctly. PORTPAL helps with that regardless of what the future holds."

### Industry Trends Worth Mentioning

- **Supply chain visibility:** COVID exposed how critical ports are to daily life
- **Labor power:** Recent strikes and contract negotiations show worker leverage
- **Pay transparency:** Workers want to understand and verify their compensation
- **Digital tools:** Even traditional industries are adopting mobile solutions
- **Pension concerns:** Workers are increasingly focused on retirement security

## 6. COMMON QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### "How did you get into this?"

**Answer:** "Honestly, by accident. I was trying to help someone understand their paycheck and realized there was no good tool for it. The more I dug in, the more I saw how complex the system is. 994 different pay combinations complex. I thought - someone needs to solve this. And since no one had, I decided to try."

*[Pivot to key message]:* "What surprised me most was the scale of the problem. These aren't people making minimum wage. They're earning $100,000+ and still can't tell if their paycheck is right."

### "How do longshoremen find you?"

**Answer:** "Mostly word of mouth. One guy shows it to another guy in the dispatch hall. Someone's spouse hears about it. It spreads through the community. We're also starting to build a presence on social media - there's actually a whole world of longshore content creators on TikTok. But the best marketing is one satisfied user telling another."

*[Add if relevant]:* "We've now got 752 active users who've tracked $41 million in earnings. That's proof the product works."

### "What's your business model?"

**Answer:** "Simple. We have a free tier that handles basic shift logging and pay calculation. For workers who want more - pay stub reconciliation, unlimited AI assistance, advanced analytics, job predictions - there's a Pro tier at $99 a year. Less than $2 a week. And one caught pay discrepancy usually pays for a full year."

*[If asked about revenue]:* "We're focused on building something that genuinely helps workers. The revenue follows when the product delivers value."

### "How do you compete with spreadsheets?"

**Answer:** "We don't really compete with spreadsheets - we compete with giving up. Most workers try spreadsheets, find them too complicated, and just stop tracking. Or they track badly and don't trust their own numbers. PORTPAL removes the friction. You log what you worked, we do the math. No formulas to maintain. No rate tables to update. It just works."

*[The killer line]:* "A spreadsheet can handle 50 pay rules. It falls apart at 994."

### "What's the biggest challenge?"

**Answer:** "Getting the rates right. The BC port pay system has nearly 1,000 unique combinations. Every time there's a rate change - which happens annually - we have to update everything. And the official documentation doesn't always match reality. Sometimes we learn the real rules from users correcting our calculations. That's actually been valuable - we've built something more accurate than official sources because we have real-world data."

*[Optional add]:* "The other challenge is reach. There are about 3,000 active longshoremen in BC. We've got 752. That's 25% - good, but not enough. We want every worker to have this tool."

### "Where do you see this going?"

**Answer:** "Three directions. First, deeper in BC - we want every longshoreman in the province using PORTPAL. Second, broader geography - there are ports in every major city, same complexity problems. Third, beyond longshoremen - any industry with complex, variable pay could use something like this. Construction, healthcare shift workers, anyone with union pay scales and variable hours."

*[If pushed]:* "But we're not trying to boil the ocean. Right now, it's about being the best tool for BC port workers. Everything else follows from that."

## 7. DIFFICULT QUESTIONS

### "Aren't longshoremen overpaid?"

**Approach:** Don't get defensive. Redirect to the complexity and value.

**Answer:** "That's a common perception, and I understand it. The pay is good. But here's the thing - the work is skilled, physical, and critical. These people move 90% of everything you buy. They work graveyard shifts in the rain, operate million-dollar equipment, and keep the economy functioning. The pay reflects that value.

What PORTPAL addresses isn't whether the pay is fair - it's whether the pay is correct. When you have 994 different pay combinations, errors happen. Workers deserve to know their paycheck matches their work."

*[If pushed harder]:* "I'm not here to debate union wages. I'm here to help workers verify they're being paid according to the contract they already have. That's fairness at the individual level."

### "Why do they need an app? Can't they just check their pay stub?"

**Approach:** Use the complexity statistics.

**Answer:** "Have you seen a longshoreman's pay stub? It's pages of line items. Each shift is a separate entry. Each entry has a rate that depends on the day, the shift, the job, the location, and sometimes the specific assignment.

To verify one shift, you need to know: Was it a weekday or weekend? Day, night, or graveyard? Which terminal? Which job classification? Any subjob variations? What's the current rate for that combination? Did overtime rules apply?

A typical worker might have 20-30 line items on a bi-weekly stub. That's 20-30 mini-calculations. PORTPAL does them automatically. Without it, you're doing forensic accounting when you should be sleeping."

### "How do unions feel about this?"

**Approach:** Position as worker empowerment, not anti-union.

**Answer:** "We haven't had any negative reaction from unions, and I think that's because PORTPAL supports union values. It helps workers understand and verify their collectively bargained wages. It doesn't undermine the union - it gives members a tool to ensure they're receiving what the contract guarantees.

If anything, informed members are stronger members. When a worker can point to specific data showing a discrepancy, that's powerful. PORTPAL gives them that evidence."

*[If asked directly about union endorsement]:* "We haven't sought formal endorsement. We've focused on building something workers actually use. The 752 users we have found us on their own merits."

### "What if your calculations are wrong?"

**Approach:** Acknowledge the risk, explain the safeguards.

**Answer:** "That's a real concern, and we take it seriously. Here's how we handle it:

First, we source our rates from official BCMEA documentation. Second, we validate against real pay data - over 71,000 shifts. When our calculation doesn't match what a user reports earning, we investigate. Sometimes we find errors in our system. Sometimes we discover undocumented variations. Either way, we learn.

We're not a substitute for the official payroll. We're a verification tool. We tell you what you should earn so you can compare it to what you did earn. If they match, great. If they don't, you have a starting point for questions.

We're also transparent about uncertainty. When we encounter a combination we haven't seen before, we flag it."

*[The honest addition]:* "Are we perfect? No. But we're more accurate than a wet notebook and a tired brain at midnight."
